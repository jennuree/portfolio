Redesigning the mobile app that Amazon deserves

Overview

In this digital era, purchasing goods and services online has become a common practice for many people. People shop online in their favorite physical stores, like Macy’s, Nordstrom, and Sephora, but also shop with online retailers, like Amazon, Ebay, and Etsy. Among all, Amazon is the largest internet retail company in the world, and they have a mobile application to bring their whole site to the tip of your fingers.

As someone that shops on Amazon religiously, I’ve ventured into probably every feature on the app. Despite the fact, I still had trouble navigating to certain features and had frustrating experiences browsing for items. After observing that other people had issues with the app too, I pursued this redesign as an opportunity to improve user experience and further my skills as a designer.

Problem:

  • Many users struggle with navigation within the app and have difficulty accessing features they normally would find on the web.

Goals:

  • To design a more intuitive and clean interface to improve user experience
  • Improve discoverability and accessibility for commonly used features
  • Allow users to browse and search for items with ease, as they would on the web.



Side note: The views from this case study are strictly my own and I am not suggesting that Amazon should adopt my redesign over their current one. This was a project born out of my own desire to further my skills as a designer. In addition, I do not have access to the data that designers at Amazon do, which may affect the design decisions that were made.

Emphathize

Learning about the audience

After hundreds of reviews, I wanted more information on specific features, user goals, and pain points...so I interviewed and surveyed a total of 157 people.

Define

Identify users’ needs

From my research, I was able to create two personas, Jessica and Derek, to help me understand how I can help users like them achieve their goal in using the app.



Persona 1: Jessica

Some insights:

  • A dedicated search tab would help users like Jessica look at products in different departments, with deals specific to the category.
  • Dark mode will reduce eye strain for users like Jessica who often browse in the dark.
  • A dedicated Compare tab will allow users like Jessica to compare multiple products of their choice at once.
Persona 2: Derek

Some insights:

  • Simplifying the home page will allow users like Derek to quickly search what he is looking for, without all the clutter.
  • The ability to sort the search results by price or popularity will make things easier for users like Derek.
  • Larger buttons on the side menu will make it easier to navigate to certain pages (Your Order!), for users like Derek who constantly check and track orders.
  • An obvious shortcut from the product page to the customer review section will save time for users like Derek, who always check reviews when ordering.



"The reviews are sometimes a pain to have to scroll to. Would be nice if they had a shortcut."
Did you know that pressing the star rating in the current app takes you directly to the customer review section? Not many people know, because the stars are so small and it is not obvious that it is a clickable area.

Ideate

Generate ideas for design!

After scoping out various shopping apps, I sketched out some ideas that could solve pain points of Amazon’s users.

How I decided on the compare tab:

(Skip this if you just want to see the final design!)




I had some ideas for how the compare tab should function…

  • The first idea was to have two items show stacked on top of each other, each with vertical scrolling to view the item as you would on the product page (left image). This would allow users to view both items simultaneously. The downside was that you could only compare 2 items, maybe 3, until you run out of screen real estate.
  • The second idea was to have “tabs” of product pages, as you would on a browser (right image). This would allow you to view more information at once, without limiting the number of products to compare to 2 or 3. The downside to this was that you couldn’t view the comparing items simultaneously.
  • The third idea that is not drawn out was to have the Home page search bar take you to the search results within the Home Tab. This meant that if you searched for another product in the Search Tab, you would see results in the Search Tab, while being able to tab back to the Home Tab to view what you searched there. I decided this would be redundant as a method to compare two products, as you would need to search for the same item twice in two different tabs. Below is the current comparison screen:

So in the end, I decided on option 2. When comparing items, I don’t want to be limited to just 2 or 3 products. What if I can’t decide between 4? Or 5? With this option, you can narrow down your options as you scroll through items all within the compare tab. Nothing will beat having multiple browsers on a desktop, but having the option to view multiple items at once on an app will improve the shopping experience for many.

Prototype

Transforming Ideas

Experience #1: Home

Instead of a side menu, I decided to use a pop out menu to put things more in reach and inserted big buttons for commonly used features, like “Your Orders.” People like the content on the home page (according to my survey), so I redesigned the layout in order to allow users to view more with minimal scrolling. The Dark Mode feature is also accessible here!

Experience #2: Dark Mode

The Dark Mode feature is easily accessible through the menu. The color is based off of Amazon’s color scheme and it will reduce strain on the eyes. This was the most requested feature from my survey.

Experience #3: Searching

Displaying products in grids with just the important information allows users to view more items at once. The dedicated search tab displays different categories for those who wish to shop by department, and includes a large button for the option to search by image, or scan (potentially increase online sales with increased discoverability!).

Experience #4: Product Page

A cleaner interface of the product page with the most important information at the top! No more scrolling through “Frequently bought together,” “Related posts,” “Sponsored products related to this…,” before getting to the customer reviews. Buttons at the top for wishlist, compare, and share, which will stay as you scroll.

Experience #5: Compare (NEW)

You can’t open tabs on an app, and the comparing options in the app right now are based off of similar items. With the compare tab, you can pick the items you want to compare and view them side by side! Since Amazon sells all sorts of items, the generated compare charts do not always show the information you want to compare. This will be an alternative way to compare outside of Amazon’s compare chart within the app.

Evaluate and test

Final Interview

With the interactive prototype and images, I asked users for their thoughts on my redesign. Here’s what people had to say:

  • “ The app looks more modern and there is less clutter on the screen”
  • “It actually looks smoother than the current amazon app, like way more cleaner and easier to use because of the layout. It’s minimalistic and I really like the dark mode“
  • “It would be nice if amazon actually used this”
  • “It looks more like amazon with their brand colors, and I like that there is a navigation bar”
  • “It’s much less list-like and more block-like which allows me to see more”
What can I do better next time?

During the process, there were things I wish I had included in my interview and survey questions that would have helped me through design decisions. Next time- think about the process more in depth and the goals of the application or website in order to ask the right questions.



I believe there’s always room for improvement, so I hope to make more interactive prototypes with different tools and develop more skills.
